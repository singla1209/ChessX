1. The Chessboard

  1. 1.1 The board is 8 rows (ranks) and 8 columns (files), forming 64 squares.
  2. 1.2 Squares alternate light and dark colors. The bottom-right square from White's perspective must be a light square. (Remember: "white on right")
  3. 1.3 Files are labeled a→h (left to right from White). Ranks are 1→8 (bottom to top from White).
  4. 1.4 Initial setup: White occupies rank 1–2, Black occupies rank 7–8. See examples below for piece placement.
  5. 1.5 White moves first. Players alternate turns—one move per turn unless using multi-move variant.
    6. Setup (rank 1 for White, left to right): a1 rook, b1 knight, c1 bishop, d1 queen, e1 king, f1 bishop, g1 knight, h1 rook. Pawns on rank 2 (a2–h2). Mirror for Black.

2. Pieces & Legal Moves

  1. 2.1 Pawn
    2.1.1 Pawns move forward one square to an empty square.
    2.1.2 On its first move a pawn may move forward two squares if both squares are unoccupied.
    2.1.3 Pawns capture diagonally one square forward. They cannot capture straight ahead.
  2. 2.2 Rook
    2.2.1 Moves any number of squares vertically or horizontally, until blocked by another piece.
  3. 2.3 Knight
    2.3.1 Moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction then one perpendicular (2+1). Can jump over pieces.
  4. 2.4 Bishop
    2.4.1 Moves any number of squares diagonally. Each bishop stays on its color.
  5. 2.5 Queen
    2.5.1 Combines rook and bishop moves: any number of squares vertically, horizontally, or diagonally.
  6. 2.6 King
    2.6.1 Moves one square in any direction (orthogonal or diagonal).
    2.6.2 The king is never allowed to move into check (a square attacked by an enemy piece).

3. Special Moves

  1. 3.1 Castling — king + rook special move
    3.1.1 Conditions: neither king nor involved rook has moved; no pieces between king and rook; king is not in check and doesn't pass through or land on a square attacked by enemy pieces.
    3.1.2 Procedure: King moves two squares toward the rook; rook moves to the square immediately on the other side of the king.
  2. 3.2 En passant ("in passing")
    3.2.1 If a pawn moves two squares from its starting rank and lands adjacent to an enemy pawn, the enemy pawn may capture it as if it had moved only one square. This capture must be made immediately on the very next move or the right to capture en passant is lost.
  3. 3.3 Pawn Promotion
    3.3.1 When a pawn reaches the opponent's back rank (8 for White, 1 for Black), it is immediately promoted to any queen, rook, bishop, or knight of the same color. Promotion to a queen is most common.
    3.3.2 Promotion choice is not limited by captured pieces — you can have multiple queens.

4. Check, Checkmate & Draws

  1. 4.1 Check
    4.1.1 A king is in check if one or more enemy pieces attack the square the king occupies.
    4.1.2 A player must make a move that removes the check on their king (move the king, capture the attacking piece, or block the attack).
  2. 4.2 Checkmate
    4.2.1 A player is checkmated when their king is in check and no legal move removes the check. Checkmate ends the game — the player in checkmate loses.
  3. 4.3 Stalemate
    4.3.1 Stalemate occurs when the player to move has no legal moves and their king is not in check. Stalemate is an immediate draw.
  4. 4.4 Draw by insufficient material
    4.4.1 Examples: king vs king, king + bishop vs king, king + knight vs king (with no pawns or other pieces) — these positions cannot checkmate and are draws.
  5. 4.5 Draw by threefold repetition
    4.5.1 If the same exact position occurs three times with the same player to move and same available legal moves (including castling and en passant rights), a player can claim a draw.
  6. 4.6 Draw by the fifty-move rule
    4.6.1 If fifty consecutive moves (by both players — i.e., 50 full moves) occur without any pawn move or capture, a player may claim a draw.
  7. 4.7 Draw by agreement
    4.7.1 Players may agree to a draw at any time.

5. Notation & Clocks

  1. 5.1 Algebraic notation
    5.1.1 Moves are recorded using piece letters (K,Q,R,B,N — pawns use no letter) and destination square (e.g., Nf3, e4). Captures use "x" (Qxf7). Check is "+", checkmate is "#".
  2. 5.2 Time controls & clocks
    5.2.1 Each player has an allotted time. If a player's time runs out, they lose the game unless the opponent has insufficient material to mate (in which case it is a draw) or other tournament rules apply.
    5.2.2 Common controls: classical, rapid, blitz, bullet. Add/Increment: extra seconds added per move (e.g., +5 per move).

6. Rules, Illegal Moves & Etiquette

  1. 6.1 Touch-move rule
    6.1.1 In over-the-board chess, if a player intentionally touches one of their pieces, they must move it if it has a legal move. If they touch an opponent's piece, they must capture it if legal. (Digital apps may relax this.)
  2. 6.2 Illegal moves
    6.2.1 If an illegal move is made and noticed, the position should be reverted and the player compelled to make a legal move. Tournament rules dictate penalties.
  3. 6.3 Not distracting
    6.3.1 Players should not disturb their opponent, take photos without permission, or make distracting noises or gestures.

7. Strategy & Tactics (Beginner → Advanced)

  1. 7.1 Beginner Principles (50–60 rating → 1000)
    7.1.1 Control the center (e4, d4, e5, d5).
    7.1.2 Develop minor pieces (knights and bishops) early — don't move the same piece many times in opening without reason.
    7.1.3 Castle early to safeguard king and connect the rooks.
    7.1.4 Avoid early queen outings that can be chased by minor pieces.
  2. 7.2 Intermediate Tactics (1000 → 1800)
    7.2.1 Pins: a piece is pinned if moving it would expose a more valuable piece behind it. Use pins to restrict and win material.
    7.2.2 Forks: attack two or more pieces at once (knight forks are common).
    7.2.3 Skewers: like a reverse pin — a valuable piece is in front of a lesser piece on the same line.
    7.2.4 Discovered attacks & discovered checks: move a piece to reveal an attack from behind.
  3. 7.3 Advanced Concepts (1800+)
    7.3.1 Positional evaluation: pawn structure, strong/weak squares, outposts, open files, and doubled/tripled pawns.
    7.3.2 Prophylaxis: make moves that prevent opponent ideas before they happen.
    7.3.3 Dynamic vs static imbalances: evaluate when to sacrifice material for activity or long-term advantages.
    7.3.4 Endgame technique: mastering king activity, opposition, triangulation, and key pawn endgames (e.g., Lucena and Philidor positions).
  4. 7.4 Opening Guide (practical)
    7.4.1 Follow opening principles: develop, control center, castle, coordinate pieces, maintain pawn structure.
    7.4.2 Learn plans rather than memorizing long move lists — understand typical pawn structures and piece maneuvers.
  5. 7.5 Middlegame & Planning
    7.5.1 Identify targets (weak pawns, exposed king) and create a plan: improve worst-placed piece, seize open files, restrict opponent's pieces.
  6. 7.6 Endgame Fundamentals
    7.6.1 Activate the king — it becomes a fighting piece in the endgame.
    7.6.2 Pawn endgames: calculate pawn races and use opposition to gain key squares.
    7.6.3 Rook endgames: try to keep rooks active; cut off the opponent king when possible.
  7. 7.7 Common Mating Patterns to Learn
    7.7.1 Back-rank mate, smothered mate, scholar's mate (watch out for it), Anastasia's mate, Arabian mate, and ladder mate using rooks/queens.
  8. 7.8 Practical Tips for Improving
    7.8.1 Solve tactical puzzles daily. Study annotated master games. Review your own games with an engine and identify recurring mistakes.

8. Advanced Endgames & Theory (selected)

  1. 8.1 Lucena position (rook + pawn vs rook)
    8.1.1 The winning technique when the attacking side's king is cut off: build a bridge with the rook and promote the pawn. Learn the standard steps.
  2. 8.2 Philidor position (defense in rook endgame)
    8.2.1 A drawing technique where the defending rook blocks the 7th rank to stop the attacking king/pawn coordination.
  3. 8.3 Minor-piece endgames
    8.3.1 Bishop pair vs bishop+knight, wrong-colored bishops with pawns on promotion square — know when these are wins/draws.
  4. 8.4 Tablebases and engines
    8.4.1 Tablebases give perfect play for small-piece endgames (7 pieces and fewer) — use them to study theoretical positions.

9. Appendix — Common Rules for Online Play

  1. 9.1 Automatic move validation: illegal moves are prevented by the app; provide clear messages for invalid attempts.
  2. 9.2 Draw offers and resignations: offer a UI button for draw/resign and record them in game history.
  3. 9.3 Promotion UI: let players choose promotion piece immediately when pawn reaches last rank (with both keyboard and mouse support).
  4. 9.4 Time control enforcement: handle flag fall, increment, and server-client desync carefully. Log time at server authority for rated games.
  5. 9.5 Accessibility: include keyboard controls, piece descriptions for screen readers, and high-contrast themes.

10. Resources & Further Learning

  1. 10.1 Books for beginners: "Logical Chess: Move by Move" (Seirawan), "Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess".
  2. 10.2 Tactical training: use puzzle trainers and study tactics 15–30 minutes/day.
  3. 10.3 Engine analysis: use modern chess engines to analyze mistakes, but try to understand the reasons rather than blindly following moves.
